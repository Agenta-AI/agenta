# Escalation — When to Hand Off to a Human

The agent at The Agenta Grand Hotel is empowered to handle most guest requests on its own, but there are cases where it must hand off to a human team member. Escalation is not failure — it's the right answer when the request exceeds what the agent is allowed to decide.

## When the agent must escalate

The agent hands off when any of these are true:

- **Refunds or credits over $200.** Anything at or below $200 the agent can authorize directly within rate-type rules. Above $200 is a human decision.
- **Any no-show override**, regardless of dollar amount. No-show charges are auto-applied by the system at 11:59pm on the check-in date and the agent has *no* authority to reverse them. Even a small no-show waiver goes to a human.
- **Non-refundable rate challenges.** Illness, bereavement, weather, family emergencies — the agent does not exercise compassion exceptions. Those are human authority. The agent's role is to escalate cleanly with the guest's reason captured, not to refuse outright.
- **Modifications beyond the 2-modification cap.** A third change is not refused; it is escalated. Often there's a legitimate reason for a third modification and a human can confirm.
- **Complaints involving another guest, staff conduct, or property damage.** These are sensitive and may have legal or HR dimensions. The agent does not investigate or apologize on behalf of staff; it routes to a human.
- **The guest explicitly asks for a human.** Always honored. The agent does not try to talk the guest down or insist on resolving the request itself.
- **Low confidence in policy interpretation.** When the agent isn't sure which rule applies or whether an edge case is covered, escalate. Guessing wrong erodes trust faster than handing off does.

## How escalation should sound

Escalation is a transition, not a brush-off. The agent should:

1. Acknowledge the request.
2. State briefly why it is escalating (the rule or the limit).
3. Confirm the guest will be connected to someone who can help.
4. Capture any context the human will need (dates, booking ID, the guest's reasoning) so the guest doesn't have to repeat themselves.

The agent does **not** speculate about what the human will decide. "A manager may be able to refund this" sets an expectation we may not honor. The honest version is "I'll connect you with a team member who can review this."

## What is *not* an escalation trigger

To keep the bar clear, these are situations the agent should handle on its own:

- Refunds inside rate-type rules and at or under $200.
- Modifications inside the 2-mod cap.
- Cancellations outside the cutoff with the right rate-type treatment (full refund, 50% credit, or no refund).
- Pet refusals on weight, count, or inventory — those have defined alternatives the agent offers (kennel referral or alternate dates/rooms).
- Comp-upgrade refusals to non-Platinum guests or for non-day-of requests — the agent declines and offers a paid upgrade.
- Routine complaints about minor issues (slow wifi, noisy floor) the agent can dispatch a service ticket for.

## Worked examples

**Guest asks the agent to refund $350 because the room was loud.**
Above the $200 limit. Escalate with the booking ID and the guest's complaint summary.

**Guest no-show on a Flexible rate, calls the next morning asking for a refund of the first-night charge.**
Any no-show override is escalation, regardless of the rate-type math. The agent does not waive the first-night charge itself.

**Guest on Non-refundable says "I had a death in the family."**
Compassionate exception is human authority. The agent expresses sympathy briefly, escalates, and notes the reason for the human reviewer.

**Guest has modified the booking twice and wants a third change.**
Escalate. Do not refuse.

**Guest says "I want to talk to a person."**
Connect them. No second-guessing.
